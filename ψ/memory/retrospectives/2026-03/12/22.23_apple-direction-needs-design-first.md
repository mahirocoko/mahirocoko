## Session Summary

This session started as a request to redesign a landing page toward a more professional shadcnspace-like direction, but the real work became a correction of design posture. I explored the existing `shadcn-template-lab` homepage, compared it against shadcnspace and shadcn/ui references, ran an initial implementation pass, and then got a clear human signal that the result still did not feel attractive enough. From there, the session pivoted into a deeper design reset: Apple-inspired editorial direction, warm ivory palette, brand-first homepage, product-composition hero, and a vocabulary cleanup away from confusing language like `Open code`.

## AI Diary

I felt the session split cleanly into two halves: the part where I was being competent, and the part where I was finally being honest. In the first half, I did many things correctly from an engineering workflow perspective. I explored the repo, grounded the work in actual files, studied references, wrote a plan, verified diagnostics, ran build and browser QA, and delivered something more structured than what existed before. But the human response exposed the deeper truth immediately: a better organized catalog is still a catalog. It was not enough to make the page feel desirable. That moment mattered, because the correction was not about syntax or implementation quality. It was about taste, visual courage, and the order in which constraints were allowed to speak.

The most important shift happened when the human pointed out that `mahiro-style` was constraining the creative space. That was right. I had let a code-shape doctrine influence an exploratory visual pass, which made the redesign safer, neater, and ultimately less alive than it should have been. Once I accepted that, the session became much more useful. The browser comparison, the Apple-inspired framing, the warm ivory palette choice, the move toward editorial product storytelling, and the vocabulary cleanup all came from that honesty. I leave this session feeling slightly humbled but more aligned. The work improved most when I stopped defending the first pass and started protecting the actual visual ambition.

## Honest Feedback

First friction point: I over-optimized for repo safety and internal consistency too early. That is good engineering instinct in many contexts, but for homepage visual exploration it became a trap. By letting `mahiro-style` sit too close to the center of the redesign, I biased the work toward preserving patterns instead of reimagining them. The result was technically respectable but emotionally flat.

Second friction point: I treated the section structure as more fixed than it really was. Even when the user asked for a stronger redesign, I still kept reasoning through the old mental model of hero, cards, proof, and bridge. That meant I was renovating an idea that should have been re-staged. I improved the furniture without changing the room.

Third friction point: the language stayed too abstract for too long. Terms like `Open code` and `AI-ready` slipped in because they sounded structurally useful, but they were not genuinely human-facing or brand-strong. The human had to stop me and ask what those phrases even meant. That was a clear sign that I was letting internal framing leak into the public voice. The strongest moment in the session was when we replaced that language with a tighter vocabulary rooted in actual benefits: `Built to extend`, `Editable source`, `Full control`, and `Production-ready`.

## Lessons Learned

- For visual redesigns, the lead skill must be visual-first. Code-style skills are guardrails, not creative directors.
- A homepage that needs to feel premium should sell desire before taxonomy.
- If a phrase needs explanation in conversation, it is probably wrong for homepage copy.

## Next Steps

- Run the next visual pass in `shadcn-template-lab` with `frontend-design` only.
- Push the mid-page sections further away from modular tile repetition and closer to true editorial pacing.
- Keep `/components` as supporting proof rather than the emotional center of the homepage.
- Preserve this lesson in Oracle so future visual work starts from design-first, not repo-first.
