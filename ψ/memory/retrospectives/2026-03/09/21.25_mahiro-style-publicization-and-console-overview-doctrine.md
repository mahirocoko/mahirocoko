## AI Diary

I felt this session as one long act of tightening signal. At the start, there was still some looseness in the thread: multiple repos, multiple skills, and a danger of treating style work as abstract taste instead of something earned from code that fought back. The useful turn came when the work stayed grounded in `haabiz-hrm-fe`. Refactoring the console overview forced a sequence of very specific judgments: how thin a route should be, when a page should compose sections directly, where mock data should live, where `t` belongs, when a file name is too repetitive for its folder, and when a helper is truly local instead of reusable. Each one of those choices sharpened `mahiro-style` more than another round of broad theory would have.

The most satisfying part was watching the doctrine change shape without losing force. I did not need to make the skill softer to make it more public. I needed to strip out source-case gravity. Once the named-project references were replaced with repo archetypes, the rules still held. That felt like the right test: if the rule only works when the reader knows Mahiro's repos, it is not doctrine yet; it is lore. This session moved several pages from lore toward method. It also reinforced that publicization is not about removing personality completely. It is about separating the portable rule from the personal source case so the rule can travel.

## Honest Feedback

Three friction points stood out. First, the session had real context breadth, and the transitions between `learn`, `trace`, implementation, and doctrine rewriting were expensive. That breadth was productive, but it also made it easier to carry hidden assumptions from one stage into another. I had to keep re-grounding in files to avoid talking from momentum instead of evidence. Second, the verification cadence was occasionally interrupted by wrapper-level tool quirks, especially around `rtk lint`. The underlying repo commands were fine, but the wrapper noise added drag and forced repeated command-path correction. Third, the doctrine publicization pass showed how easy it is to think a rewrite is done once the obvious repo names are gone. In reality, source-case flavor survives in examples, filenames, and even the shape of illustrative snippets. That means the final polish on public-facing doctrine is slower and more detail-heavy than it first appears.

The deeper lesson inside that friction is that good doctrine work is closer to editing than to coding. It needs repeated passes for wording, scope, and leakage. I cannot treat it like a one-shot refactor. The quality comes from proving that the rule survives after the original evidence has been made generic.

## Lessons Learned

- The best `mahiro-style` improvements still come from real refactor pressure, not from generalized taste.
- Public-friendly doctrine needs repo archetypes, not personal repo names, in its canonical pages.
- Route composition, owner-local `t`, and mock-data versus UI-copy separation are now stronger and clearer doctrine than they were at the start of the session.
- File-name examples and illustrative snippets can leak private source-case gravity even after obvious project names are removed.
